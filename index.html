Konfidenz

Find out if your A/B test results are real — or just coincidence.

💡 Key Concepts — In Plain English

Confidence Level

How sure are we this result is NOT just luck? 95% confidence means: if the variant truly had zero effect, you'd only get a result this extreme by random chance 5 out of 100 times. The higher, the more trustworthy.

p-value

"Probability of coincidence." If there's really no difference, how often would pure luck produce a result this extreme? p = 0.04 → 4% chance it's a fluke. Confidence = 1 − p. Lower p = more confident.

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

The skeptic's claim: "There is NO difference between control and variant. Any gap you see is random noise." Statistics tries to shoot this down. If the evidence is strong enough, we reject H₀.

Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)

The optimist's claim: "The variant actually IS different." This is what you hope to prove. You only accept H₁ when there's enough evidence to confidently reject H₀.

Z-Score

The signal-to-noise ratio of your result. How many "standard widths" is the observed difference from zero? A z-score above ~1.96 means the signal is strong enough for 95% confidence.

Uplift / Lift

How much better (or worse) is the variant relative to control? +10% uplift means the variant's conversion rate is 10% higher than control's, not 10 percentage points higher — it's relative.

Statistical Power

If a real effect exists, how likely is the test to catch it? 80% power = if the variant truly wins, you'll detect it 80% of the time. Low power = you might miss real improvements.

Multiple Variants & Bonferroni

Testing A vs B vs C raises your false-positive risk. With 3 comparisons at 95%, the true type-I error is ~14%. Bonferroni correction divides α by the number of variants to keep the total risk at 5%.
